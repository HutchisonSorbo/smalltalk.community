Part 1

Part 1: What smalltalk.community Inc Is and What We Aim to Achieve

1Our Name

We are called smalltalk.community Inc.

We are an incorporated association registered in Victoria, Australia. This means we are a legal organisation that can own property, enter contracts, and be held accountable to our community.

2What We Do (Our Charitable Purposes)

smalltalk.community Inc exists to strengthen regional communities across Victoria by bringing people together across all ages, backgrounds, and identities. We work with regional towns and shires to build connection, reduce isolation, and create intergenerational opportunities for all community members. Specifically, we:

For children (aged 5-11):

  • Create safe spaces where children can play, learn, and build friendships with peers and trusted adults
  • Support healthy development through connection, creativity, and community participation
  • Help children feel safe, valued, and part of their community
  • Create pathways for children to have a voice and be heard (age-appropriately)

For young people (aged 12-24):

  • Create safe digital and physical spaces where young people can connect with peers and trusted adults
  • Help young people develop skills, confidence, and meaningful relationships
  • Support young people to have a genuine voice in decisions affecting them
  • Build leadership capacity and resilience
  • Ensure young people have agency and are listened to (not just consulted as tokens)

For working adults (aged 25-55):

  • Create opportunities to mentor and support young people
  • Facilitate meaningful career and life connections
  • Enable professionals to contribute expertise and mentorship
  • Build workplace and community networks

For seniors (aged 65+):

  • Provide purpose, connection, and active participation
  • Create intergenerational mentoring and wisdom-sharing opportunities
  • Ensure digital accessibility and meaningful engagement
  • Reduce isolation and enable contribution

For all community members:

  • LGBTIQA+ people: Safe, affirming spaces where identity is respected and celebrated
  • First Nations peoples: Culturally safe spaces that respect Aboriginality and self-determination
  • CALD (Culturally and Linguistically Diverse) communities: Accessible, inclusive platforms that respect diverse backgrounds
  • People with disabilities: Fully accessible participation with dignity
  • Families: Intergenerational support and connection
  • Community organisations: Partnership infrastructure and collaboration opportunities
  • Professionals and service providers: Platforms to share expertise and create connections

For the whole community:

  • Build regional social cohesion and reduce isolation across all demographics
  • Create safe digital-first infrastructure with professional moderation
  • Foster intergenerational understanding and mutual respect
  • Advocate for the needs and voices of all community members in local decision-making
  • Partner with existing services to strengthen the community ecosystem
  • Share knowledge about inclusive community engagement and co-design

These purposes are charitable under Australian law because they advance the education, social, and economic wellbeing of the entire community and reduce isolation across all generations.

3We Are A Not-for-Profit Organisation

This is important: everything smalltalk.community Inc does is for community benefit, not to make private profit for members or owners.

What this means:

  • All money we raise goes back into our programs, community work, and operating costs (including fair payment for staff)
  • No member or director receives a share of our profits or owns the organisation
  • Staff and contractors can be paid fair, reasonable salaries for their work, this is normal and necessary for sustainability
  • Committee members volunteer their time and are not paid for governance duties (unless they also hold a separate staff role with clear boundaries)
  • If we ever close down, any money left goes to another charity doing similar community work, not to individual people
Part 2

Part 2: Who Can Be Part of smalltalk.community Inc

4Types of Members

We have four types of members, all essential to our inclusive community:

Full Members (aged 18+, all backgrounds and identities)

  • Community members who support what we do and want to help guide the organisation
  • Can vote on major decisions
  • Can stand for the Committee if they want to help lead
  • Have no membership fee, participation is free

Community Members and Participants (all ages, all backgrounds)

  • Children (5-11), young people (12-24), adults, seniors, families, and community members who engage with our programs and platforms
  • Have voice and influence through consultation, feedback, and advisory structures
  • Can participate in decision-making through youth forums, community advisory groups, or co-design processes
  • Are central to everything we do
  • Have no fee, participation is free

Organisational Partners

  • Schools, community organisations, services, businesses, government agencies, and social enterprises
  • Help us reach more people and strengthen community connections
  • Collaborate on programs and share resources
  • Attend meetings as partners and advisors
  • Don't have voting rights but have significant influence through partnership

Sustaining Supporters

  • Volunteers, donors, community leaders, and supporters who contribute ongoing time or resources
  • Receive updates and recognition for their contribution
  • Are valued as community connectors
  • Invited to participate in activities and advisory groups

5How to Become A Member or Participant

For Full Members (voting members, aged 18+):

  • Write to us asking to join
  • Tell us your name, contact details, and why you support our work
  • The Committee will consider your application
  • No membership fee
  • You'll hear back within 2 weeks

For Community Participants:

  • Join our programs and platforms directly
  • No application needed
  • No fee
  • Participate at whatever level feels right for you

For Organisational Partners:

  • Contact us to discuss partnership
  • Co-create an agreement about how we work together
  • This might include shared resources, co-delivery of programs, or formal collaboration
  • No fee (though we may share program costs)

For Sustaining Supporters:

  • Talk to us about how you'd like to contribute
  • Could be volunteer time, skill sharing, fundraising, or regular donations
  • We'll work out what role suits you best
  • No membership fee

6Accessibility and Inclusion Standards

All membership and participation pathways are designed to be accessible and inclusive:

  • Language: Information available in plain language and community languages as needed
  • Digital accessibility: Platforms meet WCAG 2.1 AA accessibility standards for people with disabilities
  • Cultural safety: First Nations peoples are supported through culturally informed practices and consultation
  • LGBTIQA+ inclusion: Safe, affirming spaces where all gender identities and sexualities are respected
  • Cost-free participation: No fees for any community member or participant
  • Multiple entry points: People can engage however suits them, from casual participation to formal membership
  • Physical accessibility: Meeting spaces are wheelchair accessible with parking, accessible toilets, and reasonable accommodations
  • Child safety: All spaces working with children meet Victorian Child Safe Standards

7What Members and Participants Can Do

Full Members can:

  • Attend all our general meetings
  • Vote on major decisions (one vote per person)
  • Stand for and hold positions on the Committee
  • Ask to see our documents and information
  • Raise concerns with the Committee
  • Help shape our direction through consultation

Community Participants can:

  • Come to our programs and use our platforms
  • Be part of advisory forums (Youth, Community, Senior, or topic-specific)
  • Be asked for their ideas and feedback
  • Help co-design new services and programs
  • Vote on matters directly affecting their cohort (when appropriate forums are established)
  • Feel safe, heard, and respected

Organisational Partners can:

  • Attend meetings as collaborative partners
  • Get updates about our work and opportunities
  • Provide advice and feedback
  • Co-deliver programs
  • Help connect their networks with our community

Sustaining Supporters can:

  • Participate in activities and events
  • Get regular updates about impact
  • Receive recognition and invitations to community gatherings
  • Help shape volunteer and fundraising directions

8When Participation Ends

Formal membership or participation ends if:

  • You tell us you're leaving
  • You ask us to remove your data
  • The Committee asks you to leave because your actions seriously harmed others or violated our safety standards (you get 2 weeks notice and a chance to respond)

If you disagree with being asked to leave, you can appeal to the next general meeting within 2 weeks.

We never end someone's participation due to poverty, housing status, family structure, disability, identity, age, cultural background, or inability to participate regularly.

Part 3

Part 3: How smalltalk.community Inc Is Led

9Our Leadership Team (The Committee)

smalltalk.community Inc is led by a Committee made up of volunteers from our community who care about intergenerational connection and inclusion. The Committee has:

  • Chair (leads meetings and the Committee)
  • Co-Chair (supports the Chair and steps in when needed, reflecting shared leadership)
  • Secretary (keeps records and organises meetings)
  • Treasurer (manages money)
  • Up to 5 additional members (bring different skills, perspectives, and lived experiences)

That's a maximum of 9 people on the Committee (we think this is a good size for decision-making while staying connected to community).

Diverse representation on the Committee is important. We actively encourage Committee composition that reflects the community we serve, including:

  • Young people (aged 18-24 minimum for legal reasons)
  • Seniors with lived experience
  • LGBTIQA+ identified people
  • First Nations representation
  • People from CALD backgrounds
  • People with disability experience
  • Parents and family representatives
  • Professional expertise in community, youth work, or governance

Everyone on the Committee must be at least 18 years old (mainly for legal reasons and liability).

10How Committee Members Are Chosen

Every two years at our Annual General Meeting:

  • The community is asked who they'd like on the Committee
  • People can nominate someone, and that person has to agree
  • Everyone votes for their choices
  • The people with the most votes become Committee members
  • If a position becomes empty during the year, the Committee can appoint someone to fill it until the next election

We actively recruit Committee members who reflect our diverse community and provide support so people from underrepresented groups can participate (accessibility support, training, reimbursement for costs).

11Special Protections for Our Founder

Ryan Hutchison, who founded smalltalk.community Inc, has the right to:

  • Be on the Committee for as long as they want (if re-elected or re-appointed)
  • Be asked about major decisions that affect the organisation's purpose and vision
  • Raise concerns about whether we're staying true to our founding mission
  • Have these concerns discussed at the next general meeting

Ryan can only be asked to step down from the Committee if:

  • There's a real, documented problem (like a serious breach of our values or repeated policy violations)
  • We give them 4 weeks written notice explaining the issue
  • They get a fair chance to respond
  • Two-thirds of our Full Members vote to ask them to step down

This is fair both ways: it protects Ryan's ability to shape the organisation they founded, and it prevents someone being randomly kicked out.

12What The Committee Does

The Committee shall:

  • Manage our money and property responsibly
  • Make decisions that help us achieve our charitable purposes
  • Hire and manage staff (if we have them)
  • Set policies that guide how we work
  • Make contracts on behalf of the organisation
  • Ensure we follow all the laws
  • Consult with community members on big decisions
  • Keep good records of what we do and decide
  • Report to community regularly about our progress, finances, and impact
  • Actively promote and protect the inclusion of all community cohorts
  • Ensure our platforms and programs are accessible to everyone

The Committee must:

  • Meet at least four times a year (roughly every three months)
  • Give all community members a chance to raise concerns
  • Act in the best interest of the whole community (not themselves)
  • Make sure we stay focused on our charitable purpose
  • Consult with diverse community members before major decisions
  • Regularly review whether we're truly inclusive and accessible

13Responsible Persons and Legal Duties

Committee members are called "Responsible Persons" under Australian law. This sounds formal, but it just means:

Responsible Persons must:

  • Do their job carefully and thoughtfully
  • Act honestly and in the best interests of smalltalk.community Inc
  • Not use their position for personal benefit
  • Tell the Committee if they have a personal interest in a decision (and not vote on it)
  • Follow Australian laws and ACNC requirements
  • Know what they're responsible for
  • Work to the YACVic Code of Ethical Practice principles (empowerment, participation, social justice, respect for diversity, safety, transparency)

The Committee will:

  • Train all new members about their legal responsibilities and the YACVic Code of Ethical Practice
  • Keep a register of who's on the Committee
  • Make sure no one holding position has been convicted of serious crimes or banned from being a director
  • Conduct checks before appointing new Committee members
  • Provide ongoing professional development and support

14Who Cannot Be On The Committee

Before we appoint someone to the Committee, we check that they're not:

  • Disqualified from running a charity or organisation
  • Banned by the Australian Charities and Not-for-Profits Commission (ACNC)
  • Subject to a court order banning them from holding office
  • Convicted of a serious crime involving dishonesty, violence, or child/elder abuse in the past 10 years
  • Subject to a Domestic Violence Order

We check this by:

  • Asking the person to declare they're suitable
  • Checking official government registers
  • Doing appropriate background checks (WWC Check if working with young people)
  • Seeking references

If someone becomes disqualified after being appointed, they must immediately step down.

15Removing Committee Members

A Committee member steps down if they:

  • Resign (tell us in writing)
  • Become disqualified (can't legally serve)
  • Are voted out by two-thirds of Full Members (requires 4 weeks notice and a chance to respond first)

Special rule for our Executive Director (if we have one): They can only be removed if there's documented cause (like serious misconduct), they get 4 weeks notice, and two-thirds of Full Members agree.

This keeps things fair.

16Conflicts of Interest

Sometimes a Committee member might have a personal reason to want a decision to go a certain way (like if it affects their business, family, or identity). That's called a "conflict of interest."

When a Committee member has a conflict of interest, they must:

  • Tell everyone about it before it's discussed
  • Not vote on that decision
  • Usually step out of the room while it's being discussed (unless everyone agrees they should stay)

We keep a record of all conflicts of interest.

Special note: We recognise that some Committee members' lived experience or identity (e.g., being LGBTIQA+, First Nations, disability experience) might feel personally relevant to certain decisions. That's not a conflict of interest, it's valuable perspective. A conflict is when someone has a financial or direct personal benefit at stake.

17How The Committee Makes Decisions

Meetings:

  • We give at least 7 days notice of Committee meetings
  • We need at least 50% of Committee members present to make decisions (minimum 3 people)
  • People can join by video or phone if they can't be there in person
  • Meetings are held at times accessible to our community (not just business hours)

Decision-making:

  • We decide by majority vote (more than half voting yes)
  • If there's a tie, the Chair has a casting vote (unless they have a conflict of interest)
  • We can also decide by written vote if it's urgent

Records:

  • We write down what happened at each meeting
  • We record who was there, what was discussed, and what we decided
  • Members can ask to see these minutes
  • We share summaries of key decisions with the broader community
Part 4

Part 4: Member Meetings and Having a Say

18Annual General Meeting (AGM)

Every year, usually in May-July, we hold an Annual General Meeting where all members can come together and have a say.

At the AGM, we:

  • Report on what happened this year
  • Show our financial statements
  • Elect the Committee for next year
  • Hear from the Chair and Treasurer
  • Answer member questions
  • Vote on any major changes
  • Celebrate community wins and acknowledge contributions

We'll tell you about the AGM:

  • At least 3 weeks before it happens
  • What the meeting is about
  • When and where it's happening
  • How to join (in person or online, with accessibility support available)
  • In plain language and community languages

The AGM is accessible:

  • Wheelchair accessible venue with accessible parking and toilets
  • Live captioning or Auslan interpreters
  • Child care provided
  • Online option for people who can't attend in person
  • Snacks and breaks

19Special Meetings

If something important comes up and we can't wait for the Annual Meeting, we can call a Special Meeting.

The Committee can call a Special Meeting. Or if at least 25% of Full Members ask for one (in writing), we have to call one within 3 weeks.

We'll give 2 weeks notice of what the meeting is about.

20How Voting Works

Who can vote:

  • Full Members aged 18+ can vote (one vote each)
  • Community participants can be invited to vote on matters directly affecting them (through advisory forums or specific votes)

Decision types:

  • Ordinary decisions (most things) need more than half of voting members to agree
  • Big decisions (changing the Constitution, removing a Committee member, winding up the organisation) need two-thirds to agree

How we vote:

  • Usually by a show of hands
  • Or by secret ballot if someone asks for it
  • You can't vote if your membership (if applicable) is overdue
  • We can vote electronically if we set that up
  • We can vote by proxy if someone can't attend

21Quorum (How Many People Need To Be There)

We need at least 10 Full Members at a meeting for it to be official.

If we don't have 10 people at the scheduled time, we'll wait 30 minutes then reschedule for another date. The rescheduled meeting can go ahead with whoever's there (no 10-person rule for rescheduled meetings).

Part 5: Money and Resources

22Financial Year

Our financial year runs from 1 July to 30 June.

23How We Manage Money

Basic principles:

  • All money goes toward helping our community (not personal profit)
  • We keep accurate records of all money in and out
  • We have strong checks to prevent fraud or mistakes
  • We only spend money the way members and Committee have agreed
  • Membership and community participation are free (no cost barriers to inclusion)

Bank accounts:

  • All our money is in an official bank account in the organisation's name
  • Two people have to approve any withdrawal
  • We check the bank balance every month

Spending:

  • Small regular spending is approved by the Treasurer or Committee
  • Large amounts (over $5,000) or unusual spending needs full Committee approval
  • No Committee member gets paid for being on the Committee (unless it's a special paid role like Executive Director, which has to be approved separately)
  • Any spending on programs prioritises access for people experiencing economic hardship

24Annual Financial Report

Within 4 months after the end of our financial year, we prepare a financial report showing:

  • All money we received
  • All money we spent
  • What we have in the bank
  • Any money people owe us or we owe them
  • Impact achieved with the funding

We present this at the Annual General Meeting. Members can ask questions about it.

We also follow all the rules set by Consumer Affairs Victoria (for incorporated associations) and the ACNC (for charities).

25Audits and Checks

Depending on how big we grow, we might have an external auditor or do an internal financial check.

Any audit is done by someone independent (not on the Committee). We tell all members about the results.

Part 6: How We Work Together

26Advisory Forums and Community Voice

We establish multiple advisory forums to ensure all community cohorts have voice and influence:

Youth Advisory Forum (young people aged 12-24)

  • Meets at least four times a year
  • Gives advice and feedback on programs
  • Has real say in decisions affecting young people
  • Helps co-design new services
  • Members don't have formal legal responsibility (not Responsible Persons)
  • Is supported by Committee members or staff

Children's Advisory Forum (children aged 5-11)

  • Meets quarterly at child-friendly times and locations
  • Provides age-appropriate input on programs and services
  • Helps co-design activities and spaces for children
  • Is led and supported by trained facilitators
  • Decisions are respectfully considered in Committee planning

Senior Advisory Forum (people aged 65+)

  • Meets quarterly
  • Advises on accessibility, digital engagement, intergenerational programs
  • Shares wisdom and lived experience
  • Helps recruit and mentor senior volunteers
  • Provides feedback on programs

Community Advisory Group (diverse community representatives)

  • Includes LGBTIQA+ representatives, First Nations members, CALD community members, people with disabilities, families, professionals
  • Meets quarterly or as needed
  • Advises on inclusion, accessibility, cultural safety
  • Co-designs major programs and policies
  • Ensures we're serving the whole community authentically

Professional Advisory Panel (as needed)

  • Youth workers, educators, social workers, community leaders
  • Provides expertise on evidence-based practice
  • Advises on partnerships and service integration

All advisory members receive support (payment for time if needed, accessibility support, recognition).

27Policies We'll Create and Keep Updated

The Committee will create written policies covering:

  • Child Safeguarding - How we keep children safe (Victorian Child Safe Standards compliance, Working With Children Check requirements)
  • Youth Safeguarding - How we keep young people safe (Victorian Child Safe Standards compliance)
  • Elder Dignity and Safeguarding - Protecting seniors from abuse, financial exploitation, and ensuring respectful engagement
  • Risk Management - What problems might happen and how we'll prevent or manage them
  • Accessibility and Inclusion - How we ensure universal access across all disability types, cultural backgrounds, identities
  • Cultural Safety - Specific protocols for First Nations engagement and CALD community inclusion
  • LGBTIQA+ Inclusion - Creating affirming, safe spaces, preventing discrimination
  • Conflicts of Interest - How we handle situations where someone's personal interest conflicts with the organisation's interest
  • Complaints and Grievances - How members can raise concerns or complaints
  • Privacy - How we protect people's personal information (Australian Privacy Principles compliance)
  • Fundraising - How we raise money ethically
  • Whistleblower Protection - How people can report serious problems safely and confidentially
  • Succession Planning - What happens when key people leave the organisation
  • Digital Safety and Moderation - How we keep online spaces safe and respectful

All policies are co-created with community input and reviewed annually.

28Records We Keep

smalltalk.community Inc keeps official records including:

  • Who our members and participants are
  • Who's on the Committee and when they were appointed
  • Minutes of all Committee and member meetings
  • Our financial records and bank statements
  • Contracts and agreements
  • Our policies
  • Community feedback and evaluation data
  • Incident reports and how we responded

Members and participants can ask to see these records (except confidential personal or legal information) by writing to the Secretary. We'll give you access within 10 working days.

29Community Partnership

We believe we do better work by partnering with other organisations and community members.

We maintain relationships with:

  • Local schools and educational services
  • Health and counselling services
  • Community organisations and social enterprises
  • Young people's and children's families and caregivers
  • Local and state government
  • First Nations Elders and community leaders
  • CALD community organisations
  • LGBTIQA+ support services
  • Disability support services
  • Volunteer and employment networks
  • Aged care and senior support services

We consult with these partners on major decisions and work together on programs. We recognise we're part of a wider community ecosystem, not separate from it. We actively avoid duplicating services, instead we strengthen the connections between existing support.

Part 7

Part 7: Making Changes

30Changing The Constitution

To change this Constitution, we need:

  • 3 weeks notice to all members
  • A two-thirds majority vote at a general meeting
  • We then notify Consumer Affairs Victoria within 60 days
  • If we're registered with the ACNC, we notify them too

We can change things if we need to, but we don't change our core purpose or values lightly. Major changes might include community consultation beyond just formal members.

Part 8

Part 8: What Happens If smalltalk.community Inc Closes Down

31Winding Up

If the organisation ever closes (we hope it doesn't, but we have to plan for it):

Two-thirds of Full Members have to agree it's the right decision.

All our money gets used to pay any debts.

Any money left over goes to another charity doing similar community work across all ages and cohorts (members vote on which one). If no charity is chosen, it goes to the ACNC to be used for similar charitable work.

All data about community members is securely deleted or transferred to another trusted service.

Part 9: Following Australian Charity Standards and YACVic Principles

32ACNC Governance Standards

If we register as a charity with the Australian Charities and Not-for-Profits Commission (ACNC), we commit to these standards:

Standard 1: Our Purpose

We operate solely to help all community members thrive (not for personal profit).

Standard 2: Accountability

We regularly meet with members, share information about what we do, and listen to concerns.

Standard 3: Legal Compliance

We follow all relevant laws and tell the ACNC if something serious goes wrong.

Standard 4: Suitable People

We check that Committee members aren't disqualified from holding office.

Standard 5: Duties of Committee Members

We make sure Committee members understand they must act honestly, carefully, and in the organisation's best interest.

Standard 6: Complaints

We have ways for members and participants to raise complaints and concerns.

33YACVic Code of Ethical Practice Commitment

smalltalk.community Inc adopts and commits to the YACVic Code of Ethical Practice for the Victorian Youth Sector. This guides how we engage with young people and informs our broader community practice.

We commit to the following principles:

  • Empowerment - We amplify all community members' voices and agency
  • Participation - We create genuine opportunities for all to participate in decisions affecting them
  • Social justice - We advocate for equity, human rights, and justice across all cohorts
  • Climate justice - We care for and connect with our environment
  • Respect for human dignity and worth - We respect all community members' dignity
  • Respect for diversity - We actively respect intersecting identities, cultures, abilities, and backgrounds
  • Connectedness - We build intergenerational and community connections
  • Positive wellbeing - We support all community members' abilities to thrive
  • Safety - We create environments that are physically, psychologically, and culturally safe
  • Transparency, honesty, and integrity - We're clear and consistent in our values
  • Social and ecological context - We understand how overlapping identities shape people's lives
  • Knowledge and self-care - We keep up to date with evidence and prioritise wellbeing
  • Strengths-based approach - We value community knowledge and highlight strengths

All Committee members receive training on these principles and they guide our decision-making.

34Regular Review

Every 3 years, we review our governance to check:

  • Is the Constitution still working?
  • Are we meeting ACNC standards?
  • Are we living out our commitment to inclusion and diversity?
  • Do we need to make changes?
  • What do members and community think?

We involve diverse community members in this review and tell everyone about what we find and any changes needed.

Part 10: Words We Use and Final Notes

35Definitions

  • smalltalk.community Inc = the organisation described in this document
  • Committee = the people leading the organisation
  • Members = people who are officially part of smalltalk.community Inc (Full Members)
  • Community participants = people who engage with our programs and platforms at any level
  • Responsible Persons = Committee members who help run the organisation
  • Special Resolution = at least two-thirds of voting members agree
  • Ordinary Resolution = more than half of voting members agree
  • In Writing = could be email or other electronic communication
  • Community Cohorts = all the different groups of people we serve (children, young people, adults, seniors, LGBTIQA+, First Nations, CALD, people with disabilities, families, organisations, professionals)
  • Accessible = able to be used by people with all types of disabilities and diverse needs
  • CALD = Culturally and Linguistically Diverse
  • LGBTIQA+ = Lesbian, Gay, Bisexual, Transgender, Intersex, Queer, Asexual and all other diverse gender identities and sexualities

36If Part Of This Doesn't Work

If a court decides part of this Constitution breaks the law, we remove that part and keep everything else.

37What Law Applies

If this Constitution conflicts with Victorian or Australian law, the law wins.
